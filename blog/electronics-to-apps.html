From Electronics Repair to App Development

October 2025 Journey Michael Lee Fluharty

My journey into software development started with hardware. Years of diagnosing and fixing electronics gave me a unique perspective on how systems work‚Äîboth physical and digital.

Transitioning from fixing machines to building software felt natural. The same problem-solving mindset applies, just at a different layer of abstraction. And honestly, debugging code is a lot cleaner than soldering circuit boards.

The Hardware Foundation

I graduated from ITT Technical Institute in 2004 with an Associate of Science in Computer and Electronics Engineering Technology. For the next two decades, I repaired computers and electronic systems at the component level.

This hands-on work taught me:

  • Systems thinking - How components interact to create larger systems
  • Diagnostic skills - Methodical troubleshooting and root cause analysis
  • Patience - Complex problems require careful, systematic approaches
  • Documentation - Good notes are the difference between solving a problem once or repeatedly

The Bridge to Software

Electronics repair isn't just soldering and swapping parts. Modern computer repair requires software diagnostics, firmware updates, and driver troubleshooting. I was already living in both worlds‚Äîhardware and software intertwined.

Apple's ecosystem became my laboratory. Starting as an Apple Developer in 2009, I began experimenting with iOS development as a hobby. Building apps for my own use, learning Swift, and exploring the SDK.

Why the Transition Worked

The skills transfer better than you'd think:

Debugging is Debugging

Whether you're tracing a short circuit on a motherboard or tracking down a null pointer exception, the process is the same: gather data, form hypotheses, test systematically, isolate the problem.

Documentation Matters

In electronics, you rely on datasheets and service manuals. In software, it's API documentation and technical specs. The habit of reading technical docs thoroughly was already ingrained.

Systems Thinking

A computer is a system of systems. So is an app. Understanding how components interact‚Äîwhether they're capacitors and resistors or classes and protocols‚Äîis fundamental to both disciplines.

What I Gained

Software development gave me something electronics repair couldn't: unlimited creation potential.

With hardware, I was limited by what existed. I could fix it, modify it, but I couldn't create new hardware from scratch in my home workshop.

With software, I can build anything. The only limitations are imagination and skill‚Äîboth of which can grow infinitely.

What I Kept

I brought valuable perspectives from hardware into software:

  • Reliability matters - In hardware, a single failed component brings down the whole system. I design software with the same reliability mindset.
  • Test thoroughly - You can't ship hardware fixes via update. This makes me more careful in software, even though I can patch later.
  • User impact - Broken hardware affects people's work and lives. I never forget there are real people using my apps.

The Clean Code Advantage

I wasn't kidding about debugging code being cleaner than soldering. No more:

  • Burns from hot soldering irons
  • Eye strain from microscopic surface-mount components
  • Flux fumes and chemical exposure
  • Physical tools and heavy equipment

Just a computer, a keyboard, and pure thought turned into reality. It's liberating.

Advice for Hardware People

If you're in electronics or hardware and curious about software, make the jump. Your skills translate better than you think. Start with:

  1. Pick a platform you use daily - For me, it was Apple. Build for your own ecosystem.
  2. Solve your own problems - Build tools you actually need, not theoretical exercises.
  3. Embrace the learning curve - You learned hardware from scratch. You can learn software too.
  4. Your hardware background is an asset - Understanding the full stack, from silicon to UI, makes you a better developer.

Looking Forward

I don't miss the physical toll of electronics work, but I value the foundation it gave me. Every app I build carries lessons learned from years of troubleshooting hardware.

The transition from fixing things to building things has been the best career move I've made. And it's only just begun.
